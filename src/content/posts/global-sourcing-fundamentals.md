Most first-time importers shop on unit price. Experienced buyers shop on **landed cost, lead time, and supplier redundancy**. Here is the mental model.

## Landed cost, not unit price

Unit price is roughly 40–60% of what the goods actually cost you to receive. The rest is freight, duty, port handling, inland transport, financing, and quality risk. Always model landed cost before you negotiate.

## Two suppliers per critical SKU

A single supplier is a single point of failure. For any SKU that drives more than 15% of your revenue, qualify a backup — even if you only buy 10% from them. The day your primary supplier raises prices, goes silent, or burns down, you'll thank yourself.

## Lead time as a strategic variable

A USD 0.20 cheaper unit price across a 60-day sea freight lead time often loses to a USD 0.30 unit at 35 days. Inventory carrying cost and stockout risk eat the savings.

## Where the consultant earns their fee

Negotiating MOQ, payment terms, and exclusivity windows. Most buyers leave 10–20% of value on the table because they don't know what's negotiable.
